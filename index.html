Ain't No Grave

Jamie Wilson
Written by Claude Ely (1934)  ·  from Southern Gospel Revival 1, The Modern Trade (2012)  ·  Lyrics  ·  Watch
livelive in concerttraditionalalleluia

Brother Claude Ely wrote this in 1934 at age twelve, sick with tuberculosis, after his family prayed over him. He was a Pentecostal preacher from Virginia's coal country, and the song became an Appalachian standard long before Johnny Cash recorded it as the title track of his final album. The defiance here is theological but also physical. "High hosanna" surfaces in Jamie Wilson's rendition as a fighting "holler" - confident in reaching the promised land.

Recorded live at Schneider Hall, Columbus, Texas, March 2013. Released on Southern Gospel Revival 1, a four-track EP produced by The Modern Trade. Artwork by Chris King.

Gabriel don't you blow that trumpet
until you hear from me.

As any good American mom with Irish ancestors would, mine sang this as a lullaby to her baby Daniel. The ache and sadness and war despair that Sinéad cracks open in this stripped down a capella version was always there, but the lamentation is subtext in my soul's hearing of this song. Recorded in the TV studio for the Christmas Eve 1993 edition of The Late Late Show, RTÉ. O'Connor had appeared the week before and contributed this haunting rendition. Intro trimmed.

Wade in the Water

Sweet Honey in the Rock
Traditional spiritual (pre-Civil War)  ·  Lyrics  ·  Watch
livelive in concerta cappellatraditional
† Shirley Childress Saxton, 1951–2017  ·  ASL interpreter (video only)

A pre-Civil War spiritual used as a coded instruction along the Underground Railroad — wade in the water to throw off the dogs. Sweet Honey in the Rock's a cappella arrangement strips it to pure communal voice. No instruments. Just bodies, just breath. The call-and-response structure is the oldest form of collective grief and solidarity in American music.

Recorded during sound check at Koerner Hall, Toronto, for an episode of God's Greatest Hits on Vision TV. Source: Riddle Films.

Everlasting Arms

Playing for Change ft. Dr. John
Luke Winslow-King, 2014  ·  arr. Playing for Change  ·  Lyrics  ·  Watch
studio
† Dr. John (Mac Rebennack), 1941–2019

Luke Winslow-King wrote this in 2014 — a New Orleans songwriter working in the old tradition. Playing for Change then did what they do: layered musicians from across the world into a single recording, turning a song about being held up into an act of exactly that. Dr. John is the emotional anchor — his voice, his piano, his city's long tradition of turning grief into music that insists on moving forward.

Playing for Change records musicians separately, each in their own space — a porch in New Orleans, a studio in Cape Town, a living room in Mumbai. The video is edited so they appear to be playing together in the same room. They never are, but somehow it works.

You can lean on me stranger, I believe you’ve carried too long
It’s such a long, long way, back home

Written by Dave Carter (1997)  ·  from When I Go (1998)  ·  Lyrics  ·  Watch
livelive in concert
† Dave Carter, 1952–2002

Carter wrote this in 1997 while his mother was entering the final stages of Alzheimer's disease. He finished it before she died, but never got to play it for her. He died suddenly at age 49, while on tour.

Death as a series of becomings. The live performance at the St. John Pub in Portland, December 2001, was filmed less than a year before his death.

Traditional (first recorded 1941)  ·  Decca Records, New York, November 24, 1947  ·  Lyrics  ·  Watch
studio
† Sister Rosetta Tharpe, 1915–1973  ·  † Marie Knight, 1920–2009

Tharpe, a gospel singer who stepped out from church music, but arguably not too far, really did invent rock and roll. She just didn't get the credit. This track reached number 6 on the (segregated) Billboard Race Records chart in 1948. I love the call-and-response, trading the same ecstatic line back and forth until it becomes incantation.

† Phoebe Snow, 1950–2011

Phoebe Snow's debut single in 1974 was a massive hit. In 1975, the same year this was recorded, her daughter Valerie was born disabled. Snow largely stepped back from performing for decades to care for her. This duet, full of hope that a long streak of bad luck is gone at last, charts a course for all of us.
